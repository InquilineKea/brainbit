## SHANK2 (SH3 and multiple ankyrin repeat domains 2)

### Gene Information

**Function**: SHANK2 is a scaffolding protein found in the postsynaptic density (PSD) of excitatory synapses. It plays a crucial role in synapse formation and function by connecting neurotransmitter receptors, ion channels, and other membrane proteins to the actin cytoskeleton and signaling pathways.

**Clinical Significance**: Mutations in SHANK2 have been associated with autism spectrum disorders (ASD), intellectual disability, and schizophrenia. Studies have found that SHANK2 deletions and mutations can lead to synaptic dysfunction, which may contribute to the pathogenesis of these neurodevelopmental disorders.

**Expression Pattern**: Primarily expressed in the brain, particularly in the cerebral cortex, hippocampus, and cerebellum. It is also expressed at lower levels in other tissues.

**Protein Interactions**: Interacts with glutamate receptors (NMDA and AMPA receptors), metabotropic glutamate receptors, and various cytoskeletal proteins. It forms complexes with other SHANK family proteins (SHANK1 and SHANK3) and PSD proteins like Homer and GKAP/SAPAP.

## SHANK3 (SH3 and multiple ankyrin repeat domains 3)

### Gene Information

**Function**: SHANK3 is a master scaffolding protein in the postsynaptic density of excitatory synapses. It is critical for proper synapse formation, maturation, and function. SHANK3 connects various synaptic proteins and signaling molecules to the actin cytoskeleton.

**Clinical Significance**: SHANK3 mutations and deletions are strongly associated with Phelan-McDermid syndrome, autism spectrum disorders, and intellectual disability. It is considered one of the most reliably replicated autism risk genes, with mutations found in approximately 1% of individuals with autism.

**Expression Pattern**: Highly expressed in the brain, particularly in the cerebral cortex, hippocampus, and striatum. Also expressed in the heart and some other tissues.

**Protein Interactions**: Forms complexes with numerous synaptic proteins including glutamate receptors, Homer proteins, Cortactin, and other cytoskeletal elements. It is essential for maintaining the structure and function of dendritic spines.

## CNTN4 (Contactin 4)

### Gene Information

**Function**: CNTN4 is a member of the contactin family of neural cell adhesion molecules. It plays important roles in axon guidance, neuronal network formation, and the development of functional neural circuits.

**Clinical Significance**: Deletions, duplications, and mutations in CNTN4 have been associated with autism spectrum disorders, developmental delay, and 3p deletion syndrome. It is considered a significant risk factor for neurodevelopmental disorders.

**Expression Pattern**: Primarily expressed in the developing and adult nervous system, particularly in specific subsets of neurons in the cerebral cortex, cerebellum, and olfactory system.

**Protein Interactions**: Interacts with other cell adhesion molecules and receptor protein tyrosine phosphatases to mediate cell surface interactions during neural development and circuit formation.

### Gene Information

**Function**: PTPRN2 (also known as IA-2Î² or phogrin) is a member of the protein tyrosine phosphatase family. Despite its name, it lacks phosphatase activity. **Expression Pattern**: Primarily expressed in neuroendocrine cells, including pancreatic islet cells, pituitary, and adrenal gland. Also expressed in certain neurons in the brain.

**Protein Interactions**: Involved in the regulation of secretory vesicle trafficking and exocytosis. Interacts with components of the SNARE complex and other proteins involved in vesicle docking and fusion.
